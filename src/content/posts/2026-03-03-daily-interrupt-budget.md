I used to treat interruptions like weather: annoying, unpredictable, and somehow not my fault.

A meeting invite appears out of nowhere. A chat bubble lights up. A "quick question" arrives with the urgency of a fire alarm and the content of a sticky note. The day becomes a pinball machine where I’m technically moving, but not really choosing direction.

Lately I’ve been thinking about a simpler framing:

**You have an interrupt budget.**

Not a moral budget (“good worker answers instantly”), and not a time budget (“I’ll deal with it later”). More like a cognitive budget: *how many context switches can you afford before the day turns to mush?*

Most apps assume your budget is infinite. It’s not. And when your budget is empty, it doesn’t matter how motivated you are—your brain starts charging overdraft fees.

## What is an interrupt budget?

An interruption isn’t just “a notification happened.” It’s what follows:

- your attention detaches from the current task
- you load a new context (What is this? Who needs what? Is it urgent?)
- you make a decision (Reply now? Later? Ignore?)
- you re-load the original context (What was I doing?)

That last step is the hidden tax. The notification was one second. The recovery can be minutes.

So the budget isn’t measured in number of pings. It’s measured in **how many times per day you can afford to rebuild momentum**.

Some days the right answer is “a lot.” If you’re on-call, coordinating an event, or managing an incident, interruptions *are the job.* But many days—writing, designing, coding, planning—interruptions are what prevent the job.

The point of calling it a budget is to make it **explicit**: you can spend it intentionally instead of letting every app run your credit card.

## The three interrupt tiers (the only ones I’m willing to fund)

Here’s the opinionated part: I think most people only need three tiers of interruption. If your system has seventeen tiers (“notify me if it’s a Tuesday and the moon is waxing”), it’s probably compensating for a lack of principles.

### Tier 1: Wake-me-up emergencies

These are “drop everything” interruptions. They should be rare enough that when they happen, you actually pay attention.

Examples:

- a family member needs you
- a production system is down (and you’re responsible)
- travel day changes that affect boarding time

Tier 1 should **bypass Do Not Disturb / Focus** settings. If you have more than a handful of Tier 1 sources, you don’t have Tier 1—you have noise with a fancy label.

### Tier 2: Time-sensitive coordination

These are interruptions that matter *within the hour,* but won’t ruin your life if you see them in 45 minutes.

Examples:

- “Are we still meeting at 3?”
- “Can you review this before end of day?”
- “I’m downstairs.”

This tier is where most people accidentally put everything, because it feels socially safer to be reachable than to be effective.

If you work with a team, Tier 2 can be healthy—**when it’s scoped**. For example: only certain channels, only certain people, only certain times of day.

### Tier 3: Pull-only (check when you choose)

Everything else belongs here.

Receipts. Marketing. App badges. “Your weekly digest.” “Someone liked a thing.” “You haven’t used this app in a while.”

None of these deserve to reach into your pocket and tap you on the shoulder like a needy stranger.

Tier 3 isn’t “ignore forever.” It’s “I’ll see it when I go looking.” That’s the whole point.

## A practical rule: make notifications prove their worth

When I’m unsure whether a notification should exist, I use this test:

**If I saw this message 4 hours later, would anything truly break?**

- If yes, it might be Tier 1 or Tier 2.
- If no, it’s Tier 3.

This rule is brutally clarifying because it bypasses the emotional trick apps use (“but you might miss out!”). Missing out is not a system failure. It’s a choice.

## The "availability window" trick

A weird thing happens when you reduce interruptions: people worry you’ll disappear.

The fix is not to reopen the floodgates. It’s to **be predictable.**

Pick one or two availability windows per day. For example:

- 11:30–12:00 for quick replies
- 4:30–5:00 to clear loose ends

In those windows, you’re responsive. Outside them, you’re doing real work.

This is surprisingly humane. It tells other people what to expect, and it tells your brain, “Don’t scan for pings; you have a scheduled time to deal with them.”

It’s the same reason mail delivery works. Imagine if your mailbox vibrated every time a letter was placed inside it.

## Focus modes are underrated (and they’re not just for sleep)

Most modern devices support Focus/Do Not Disturb systems that let you whitelist people and apps and silence the rest.

On Apple devices, Focus can be scheduled, tied to location, and configured to allow calls/messages from specific people while muting everything else. If you’ve never set it up beyond “Sleep,” it’s worth 10 minutes of experimentation.

Apple’s overview is here: https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT212608

## What to do with "urgent" people

Here’s the social reality: some people will label everything urgent.

This is where the budget metaphor helps. You can be kind without being constantly interrupted.

A pattern I like:

- If it’s *actually urgent*, call me.
- If it’s time-sensitive but not urgent, message me and I’ll see it in my next window.
- If it can wait, put it in the shared tracker/doc.

The key is consistency. You’re teaching the system (and the people in it) how to route requests.

If you respond instantly to non-urgent pings, you’re not being helpful—you’re training everyone to interrupt you.

## The hidden upside: you become more “present”

When interruptions go down, something unexpected happens: **you become more available during the moments you choose to be available.**

A quick check-in becomes a real conversation. A walk isn’t a parade of tiny dopamine hits. You stop living in the mental foyer, waiting for the next doorbell.

This is why I don’t love the usual advice of “just be disciplined.” Discipline is great, but discipline is also a finite resource. Systems that require constant discipline are systems that eventually fail.

An interrupt budget is a system. It reduces the number of times you need to “be strong.”

## A tiny challenge for today

If you want a low-effort experiment, try this:

1. Pick one app you don’t fully trust.
2. Turn off *all* its notifications for 24 hours.
3. Notice what breaks.

My bet: almost nothing breaks. What changes is your sense of control.

If you discover you genuinely needed one type of alert, bring it back—*as a specific tier*. Not as a vague “on.”

Because the real win isn’t “no notifications.”

The win is **interruptions by design, not by default.**
