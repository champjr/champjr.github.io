Every January, the internet becomes a stadium full of people yelling the same three plays:

1. “New year, new me.”
2. “This time I’ll totally keep it up.”
3. “Wait, why am I already behind?”

I’m not here to dunk on resolutions. I love the idea of a fresh start. I just think we routinely choose the hardest possible implementation: rewrite your operating system in one weekend.

Instead, I want to steal a move from software engineering: **the Strangler Fig pattern**.

If you haven’t run into it, the concept is simple and surprisingly gentle. You don’t rip out the old system and replace it all at once. You **wrap** it. You create a new path around it, route a little traffic through the new path, and keep expanding until the old thing becomes irrelevant.

Martin Fowler has a classic write-up of this idea for software, and it’s worth a skim even if you don’t write code: https://martinfowler.com/bliki/StranglerFigApplication.html

Here’s the twist: I think the same approach is the most reliable way to change **personal workflows**.

## The problem with “workflow rewrites”

Most workflow changes fail for boring reasons:

- **You underestimate the surface area.** The thing you want to change touches a dozen habits, a couple tools, and your calendar.
- **You overestimate the value of novelty.** New systems feel effective before they are effective.
- **You try to delete the old system.** But the old system isn’t just a tool. It’s a set of reflexes.

When people say “I’m switching to a new task app,” what they often mean is “I want the emotional sensation of being caught up.”

Relatable! But if you switch everything at once, you don’t just change the UI. You break the muscle memory that kept you moving on tired days.

So the goal is not “become a different person by Monday.” The goal is:

> **Keep shipping your life while you swap out the scaffolding.**

## What “strangling” a workflow looks like

Let’s define the old system:

- It’s messy.
- It exists in multiple places.
- It is partly in your head (the most unreliable storage medium, frequently corrupted by vibes).
- But it’s *working enough* to keep you employed and fed.

A strangler-style change says: keep the old system running. Add a thin new layer that captures only a small slice of behavior.

Here are a few examples.

### Example 1: Don’t replace your task manager. Add an “intake gate.”

If your tasks are currently scattered across:

- sticky notes
- a notes app
- a task app
- email flags
- “I will remember this” (you will not)

…then switching task apps doesn’t fix the real problem. The problem is **intake**.

A strangler move is: create a single intake rule.

- Pick one place where *new tasks are allowed to land*.
- Everything else becomes read-only (not deleted, just not the official inbox).

That’s it. No grand migration. No “import all my lists.”

You can keep the old places for reference while you route new items through the gate. Over a couple weeks, you’ll notice something magical: the new system starts to feel “complete” without ever doing a heroic cleanup.

### Example 2: Don’t “start journaling.” Add a 30-second daily log.

“Journal every day” is a lovely aspiration and a fantastic way to create guilt.

Try a strangler version:

- Create one note titled something like `Daily Log`.
- Each day, add exactly three bullets:
  - **What I did** (a tiny factual list)
  - **What I learned** (one sentence)
  - **What’s next** (one decision)

The key constraint: **30 seconds**.

You’re not trying to capture your inner world. You’re creating a low-friction trace. Later, if you want to expand a day into real writing, you can. But the first win is simply making your past self searchable.

### Example 3: Don’t “become organized.” Strangle one recurring meeting.

Recurring meetings are workflows disguised as calendar blocks.

Pick one meeting you attend weekly. Add a tiny wrapper:

- A standing agenda doc with three sections:
  - Decisions needed
  - Updates (bullets only)
  - Open questions
- A rule: if something doesn’t fit those headings, it doesn’t go in.

You haven’t reorganized your life. You’ve just created a narrow channel where information can flow without turning into fog.

Do this for one meeting for a month and you’ll have evidence that structure isn’t oppressive — it’s oxygen.

## The “radius of trust” principle

There’s a psychological reason this works: it respects your current **radius of trust**.

When you change a workflow, you’re making a bet. You’re betting that the new system will still be usable when:

- you’re tired
- you’re rushed
- something unexpected happens
- you miss a day

Big rewrites demand trust you haven’t earned yet.

A strangler change starts with tiny bets:

- “Can I capture new tasks in one place for a week?”
- “Can I write three bullets per day for ten days?”
- “Can I keep a single meeting doc tidy?”

When you succeed at small bets, you increase the radius of trust. Then you can safely expand.

## How to actually do it (a checklist)

If you want to try this today, here’s a practical recipe.

1) **Name the legacy system without insulting it.**

Write down what you do right now, neutrally. “Tasks are in Apple Notes and email.” “I plan my week on Sunday night.” “I remember deadlines by panicking.” (Okay, maybe a tiny insult.)

2) **Choose one interface to wrap.**

Not “my whole life.” Pick one interface:

- task intake
- meeting notes
- weekly planning
- writing drafts
- reading highlights

3) **Define a narrow success condition.**

Examples:

- “All new tasks go into one inbox.”
- “I write three bullets daily.”
- “I add a next action to any meeting note.”

4) **Make failure cheap.**

If you skip a day, the system should still work tomorrow.

This is the part most people miss. If your workflow can’t survive a bad Tuesday, it’s not a workflow; it’s a performance.

5) **Only migrate when you feel friction, not when you feel shame.**

Migration is a tool, not a penance.

If you keep opening the old notes app because “the real list is still there,” that’s a signal to migrate that specific chunk. But don’t migrate as a way to prove you’re serious. Migrate as a way to reduce switching costs.

## What this looks like over time

The surprising thing about strangler-style change is that it doesn’t feel dramatic. It feels almost boring.

Week 1: you add a wrapper.

Week 2: you route new items through it.

Week 3: you notice the old system is mostly historical.

Week 4: you migrate one category because you’re tired of checking two places.

At some point, you look up and realize the “new system” won without a fight.

And you didn’t have to become a different person.

## The real win: you keep your momentum

The best workflows aren’t the prettiest. They’re the ones that survive contact with real life.

If you’re staring at a messy system and thinking, “I should really start over,” consider a smaller move:

- wrap the mess
- redirect a little traffic
- expand the wrapper as trust grows

You’re not deleting the past. You’re building a path out of it.
