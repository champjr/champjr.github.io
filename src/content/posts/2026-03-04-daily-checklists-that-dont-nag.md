Checklists have a branding problem.

In theory, they’re about reliability: you write down the few critical steps that are easy to forget, then you stop forgetting them. In practice, a lot of personal checklists turn into something else entirely:

- a guilt machine
- a second job
- a long document you avoid because it reminds you of all the ways you’re not a “systems person”

And then you conclude, “Checklists don’t work for me.”

I think checklists *do* work for you. You’ve just met the wrong kind.

Here’s my take: **the best checklists are not schedules. They’re guardrails.** They exist to prevent the dumb, avoidable failures—the ones that feel especially insulting because you *absolutely* knew better.

(You knew better. Past-you just didn’t have a clipboard.)

## The two kinds of checklists

Let’s split the world into two types.

### 1) Guardrail checklists (good)

These are short lists that prevent mistakes during a repeatable process.

Examples:

- “Before you travel: passport, charger, headphones.”
- “Before you ship a change: run tests, update version, write release notes.”
- “Before you publish a post: scan for secrets, broken links, obvious typos.”

Guardrails are useful because they help in moments when your brain is predictably unreliable: when you’re rushed, excited, tired, or switching contexts.

### 2) Identity checklists (usually bad)

These try to manufacture a lifestyle. They don’t prevent a failure mode; they define a person.

Examples:

- “Daily: meditate, journal, stretch, read 50 pages, drink 3 liters, practice Italian, inbox zero.”
- “Morning routine: 19 steps, no deviations.”

If you can do these, great. But for most of us, these lists don’t create habits—they create **backlog shame**.

Identity checklists also have a subtle flaw: if you miss one item, the whole thing feels broken. And once a checklist is broken, people abandon it rather than edit it.

Guardrails survive missed days. Identity checklists interpret missed days as moral failure.

## A checklist should be shorter than your patience

This is my first rule:

> **A checklist must be short enough that you will still do it on a grumpy Tuesday.**

If it only works on your best day, it’s not a checklist. It’s a fantasy.

A good personal checklist is often **5–9 items**. Not because there’s magic in those numbers, but because that’s roughly the size of “annoying but doable.”

Once a checklist grows beyond that, it stops being a tool and becomes a document.

If you have 27 things you “should” do before publishing, you don’t need a longer checklist.

You need to:

- automate more of it
- decide what doesn’t matter
- or split it into smaller checklists triggered by specific situations

## The secret: write checklists around failure modes

The fastest way to create a checklist you’ll actually use is to build it from pain.

Instead of asking, “What is the perfect process?” ask:

- What are the *three* mistakes I keep repeating?
- What goes wrong when I’m rushed?
- What’s the embarrassing thing I do once a month?

Those answers are your checklist items.

For example, if you keep publishing posts with:

- one broken link
- a missing tag
- or a sentence that clearly got written at 11:58pm

…those are not character flaws. They’re failure modes.

A guardrail checklist is just a list of failure modes with countermeasures.

## Don’t make the checklist do the thinking

A personal checklist should not contain items like:

- “Be thoughtful.”
- “Write better.”
- “Make it great.”

Those aren’t steps. They’re aspirations.

Checklist items should be **binary and observable**:

- “Run `npm test`.”
- “Open the preview.”
- “Search for `TODO`.”
- “Read the intro out loud.”

If you can’t tell whether you did the item without negotiating with yourself, the item is too vague.

This is one reason professional checklists (in aviation, medicine, etc.) are so effective: they are designed to be executed under pressure by humans with limited attention.

Atul Gawande’s *The Checklist Manifesto* is a great read on why checklists work in complex environments and how they’re designed to be usable (not just correct):

https://atulgawande.com/book/the-checklist-manifesto/

## The “two-layer” checklist: core + optional

Here’s a practical pattern that keeps checklists from turning into nag lists.

Create two sections:

**Core (must-do, always):**

- 5-ish items
- protects you from real mistakes
- fast

**Optional (nice-to-have):**

- the polish
- the “if I have time” stuff
- anything that shouldn’t block you from finishing

The trick is psychological: when you’re tired, you can still complete the core list and feel done.

Optional items can be ignored without guilt. That’s the whole point.

If optional items keep getting skipped, promote the important ones to core and delete the rest. A checklist is allowed to evolve.

## The best checklists are triggered, not scheduled

Scheduling a checklist (“every day at 7am, do the checklist”) often turns it into an identity list.

Instead, attach checklists to **events**:

- “When I book travel, run the travel checklist.”
- “When I open a PR, run the shipping checklist.”
- “When I hit publish, run the publishing checklist.”

Triggered checklists don’t nag. They appear when the risk appears.

This is how checklists stay relevant and lightweight: they’re present at the moment of highest leverage.

## A tiny example: the “publish something” checklist

Here’s a personal template that works for a lot of creative output (blog posts, docs, videos, even long emails). Adjust to taste:

**Core:**

- Read the first paragraph once (does it *start*?)
- Scan for obvious secrets/PII (names, emails, tokens, addresses)
- Check links (click them or run a link checker)
- Preview the final output (not just the editor)
- Hit publish

**Optional:**

- Add a better title
- Add one useful link
- Add a visual
- Cut 10% of the words

Notice what’s missing: “write a masterpiece.”

The checklist doesn’t exist to make you brilliant. It exists to make you **less fragile**.

## The real benefit: you stop re-learning the same lesson

There’s a quiet tax in personal work: the cost of re-learning.

You forget a step, you pay for it, you swear you’ll remember next time, and then next time you forget again because “next time” is always busy.

A checklist is the cheapest way to turn a lesson into a system.

It’s like leaving yourself a note *in the exact place you’ll need it later.* Not because you’re dumb, but because you’re human.

(And humans are basically attention-powered squirrels.)

## A five-minute challenge

If you want to make this real today, do this:

1. Pick one repeatable process you do at least weekly (publishing, invoicing, sending a report, packing, cleaning up after a project).
2. Write down the **three mistakes** you most often make in that process.
3. Turn each mistake into one binary checklist item.
4. Put the checklist where the process happens (a note pinned to the top, a template, a snippet, a sticky note—whatever).

That’s it. Three items.

If you use it even twice, you’ve already won. Because the bar isn’t “become a checklist person.”

The bar is: **stop paying for the same mistake over and over.**
