There are two kinds of interruption days.

1. The kind where you *choose* to do a bunch of small things.
2. The kind where small things choose *you*, one after another, like seagulls spotting an unguarded French fry.

On those second days, the problem usually isn’t that any single request is unreasonable. It’s that your brain keeps trying to hold onto the previous task while also responding to the new one. You end up running a tiny background process of anxiety: *don’t forget what I was doing… don’t forget what I was doing…*

I’ve found one simple habit that helps more than it has any right to: **the Parking Lot Note**.

It’s not a productivity system. It’s not a second brain. It’s not even particularly aesthetic.

It’s just a place to park the thing you’re about to lose.

## The idea

When you get interrupted (or you interrupt yourself), your brain tries to keep a “resume point” in memory:

- what you were working on
- where you were in it
- what you planned to do next
- what you need to look up

The trouble is that human RAM is… not amazing. Especially when Slack/email/your own curiosity keeps poking it.

The Parking Lot Note is a single note (paper or digital) where you write down *just enough* to safely drop the current thread.

The goal isn’t to document your life.

The goal is to let your brain stop spinning.

## The format (steal this)

Create a note called **Parking Lot**. Keep it pinned, favorited, or physically on your desk.

When you get interrupted, add one bullet using this template:

- **NOW:** <what I was doing> → **NEXT:** <the very next action> (optional: **BLOCKED BY:** <thing>)

Examples:

- **NOW:** editing the blog post intro → **NEXT:** add the example about “seagull tasks”
- **NOW:** debugging login redirect → **NEXT:** add log line around callback handler (**BLOCKED BY:** need prod repro)
- **NOW:** reading docs → **NEXT:** search for “rate limit headers” and copy snippet into notes

That’s it.

The magic is in the *NEXT* part. “Keep working on it later” is not a next action; it’s a polite lie.

A next action is concrete:

- “Open file X and change Y.”
- “Run command Z.”
- “Write the paragraph that starts with ____.”

If your note doesn’t tell Future You exactly what to do, Future You will instead open 12 tabs, forget why, and start a new hobby.

## Why it works (and a tiny bit of science)

Interruptions hurt for two reasons:

1. **They break attention.**
2. **They create “open loops.”** Your brain keeps rehearsing unfinished stuff so it won’t disappear.

Writing a quick resume point is a way of closing the loop *enough* that your brain stops babysitting it.

Also, the cost of switching is real. A frequently-cited study by Gloria Mark (UC Irvine) found that after an interruption, it can take **about 23 minutes** on average to return to the original task.

Source: https://www.ics.uci.edu/~gmark/CHI2005.pdf

That number isn’t a moral judgment. It’s just physics (the “human brain” kind, not the “falling objects” kind).

So the Parking Lot Note is basically you saying: *Hey brain, I copied the state to disk. You can stop keeping it in registers.*

## The rules (so it stays lightweight)

I’ve tried versions of this that got too fancy. They always collapse under their own organizational gravity.

These rules keep it alive:

### 1) One note, not a system

If you create separate parking lots for work, personal, reading, errands, dreams you had in 2014, etc… congratulations, you’ve built a system that requires maintenance. Maintenance is how good habits die.

One note.

### 2) Capture only at interruption points

Don’t use it as a daily log.

Use it at the exact moment you’re about to context switch.

That’s when it pays rent.

### 3) Make “NEXT” painfully specific

If you can’t make it specific, write the question:

- **NEXT:** figure out why the redirect loops (start by searching for “302” in logs)

A question is better than a vague promise.

### 4) Review it at natural boundaries

You don’t need a weekly review ritual with candles and a carefully curated lo-fi playlist (unless that’s your thing).

Just glance at the note when you:

- finish a meeting
- close your laptop for lunch
- wrap up for the day

When you pick one item back up, delete it from the list.

The act of deletion is underrated. It’s proof to your brain that things actually finish.

## What about “urgent” interruptions?

If something is genuinely urgent, you probably don’t need this; you’ll remember because your nervous system will tattoo it onto your soul.

The Parking Lot Note is for everything else:

- the medium-urgent request that isn’t urgent *right now*
- the “quick question” that turns into a 40-minute detour
- the “I should totally…” thought that appears while you’re trying to write a sentence

It’s also great for the interruptions you do to yourself. (I am looking directly at the part of my brain that loves reorganizing folders instead of writing.)

## A small upgrade: two buckets

If you want one tiny enhancement without turning this into a lifestyle brand, split the note into two sections:

- **RESUME** (things you were actively doing when interrupted)
- **LATER** (ideas, rabbit holes, “wouldn’t it be cool if…”)

The key distinction is emotional.

- RESUME items are promises to Past You.
- LATER items are souvenirs from your wandering mind.

Treat them differently.

## The surprising benefit: calmer communication

There’s a social angle to this too.

When you have a reliable place to park things, it’s easier to respond to someone without panic-agreeing:

- “Yes, I can do that” (meaning: I will now forget, feel guilty, and do it at midnight)

Instead, you can say:

- “Got it — I’m in the middle of X. I’ll park this and circle back after Y.”

That’s not a rejection. It’s a boundary with a plan.

## Try it today

If you want to test this with minimal ceremony:

1. Create a note titled **Parking Lot**.
2. The next time you’re about to switch tasks, add one bullet with **NOW → NEXT**.
3. At the end of the day, pick *one* item from the list to finish or schedule.

That’s enough to feel the difference.

Will it prevent interruptions? No.

But it can stop interruptions from turning your day into a browser with 47 tabs and no idea which one is playing music.
